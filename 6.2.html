Global Ice Cream Market

Ice cream is a universally loved product, with infinite flavor possibilities and form factors, making it a dynamic global industry. The international market, valued at billions, is led by major players like Ben & Jerry's, Häagen-Dazs, and Unilever’s Magnum, but consumer curiosity drives demand for innovative brands.

Bar chart: Sales of top ice cream brands in the US (2023), reflecting global influence.

Major International Players

The global ice cream market is dominated by legacy brands, primarily under conglomerates like Unilever and Nestlé, which have shaped the industry for decades.

Top Global Brands (US Sales, 2023):

  • Ben & Jerry's (Unilever): $951M
  • Häagen-Dazs (Nestlé/Froneri): $802M
  • Blue Bell: $719M
  • Breyers (Unilever): $546M
  • Dreyer's (Nestlé/Froneri): $546M
  • Tillamook: $324M
  • Turkey Hill: $248M
  • Talenti (Unilever): $209M

These brands leverage global distribution networks, but low switching costs allow consumers to explore newer entrants, driving competition.

Private Equity in Global Markets

The global appeal of ice cream has attracted significant private equity and venture capital, transforming small scoop shops into premium brands with international ambitions.

Notable Investments in Emerging Brands:

  • Van Leeuwen: $25M
  • Salt & Straw: $4M
  • Jeni's: $15M
  • Oberweis: $20M
  • Ample Hills Creamery: $19M

Investors bet on these brands to capture the next generation of consumers, but thin margins and high operational costs pose challenges.

Challenges of Global Expansion

Despite its universal appeal, the ice cream business faces significant hurdles in international markets. Conglomerates like Unilever and Nestlé have struggled with low margins, leading to divestitures.

  • Unilever: Scaled to the world’s largest ice cream company by 2001, but its ice cream division grew only 4% from 2001 to 2023 (€7.6B to €7.9B). It plans to sell the division by 2025 due to low margins.
  • Nestlé: Peaked in 2007 with \$8.27B in ice cream sales but sold its division to Froneri in 2019 for \$4B, citing focus on high-growth verticals.
  • Froneri: Increased revenue but maintains a low 10% margin, with operating income offset by debt interest.
  • Kroger: Sold Turkey Hill in 2019 at a 50% discount, reflecting market stagnation.

Retail chains like Baskin-Robbins and Cold Stone also face stagnation, with Baskin-Robbins’ international sales growing only 14% per store over 12 years.

Case Studies in International Markets

Sam's Fried Ice Cream (USA)

Located in New York’s Lower East Side, Sam’s Fried Ice Cream capitalizes on the novelty of fried ice cream, sourcing bulk ice cream from Hershey’s and selling at $8 per portion. Its small 200-square-foot shop and street fair presence demonstrate a lean, opportunistic approach.

Business Breakdown:

  • Shop Revenue: \$81,000 annually (\$15,000/month in summer, $4,000/month otherwise).
  • Street Fair Revenue: $120,000 annually.
  • Total Revenue: $201,000.

Smoove Ice Cream (USA)

Smoove, based in Manhattan with a new California location, targets young consumers with Asian-inspired flavors (Ube, Matcha). It diversifies with croffles and boba floats to combat seasonality, leveraging social media and events like Anime Con.

Business Breakdown:

  • Shop Revenue: \$462,121 annually (\$65,000/month in summer, $30,000/month in winter).
  • Total Revenue (Shop & Events): $531,000.
  • Profit Margins: 13% average (10% winter, 15% summer).

il laboratorio del gelato (USA)

This B2B-focused gelato supplier in Manhattan serves 400 restaurants with 250+ flavors, maintaining quality without compromising on ingredients. Its retail storefront sells unique flavors, mitigating seasonality through consistent restaurant demand.

Business Breakdown:

  • Wholesale Revenue: $2,300,000 annually.
  • Retail Shop Revenue: $480,000 annually.
  • Total Revenue: $2,780,000.
  • Profit Margins: 10% wholesale, 9% retail.

Conclusion

The global ice cream market is both promising and challenging. While consumer demand remains strong, low margins and operational complexities have driven major conglomerates to exit, creating opportunities for innovative brands like Hocco. Passion, differentiation, and strategic partnerships are key to thriving in this competitive landscape.
