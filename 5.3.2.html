Practical Insights & Examples from the Transcript

1. MVP (Minimum Viable Product)

Not just a prototype — it must solve a real problem and provide value.

Should be viable enough to test adoption, not just an idea on paper.

2. Angel Investors vs. VCs

Angels: Write smaller checks (e.g., $20k–$50k), often part-time, sometimes as a hobby.

VCs: Manage large funds, write big checks, want significant ownership (~20%), often take board seats.

3. Profitability

Example: Google is “wildly profitable” through ads.

Startups don’t need to be profitable early, but mature companies are expected to be.

4. Burn Rate

If you start with $1M and end with $900k, burn = $100k.

Cash management is critical: Run out of money = game over.

5. Funding Rounds

Seed: Flexible in size and structure.

Series A: Usually a major investor steps in, takes ~20% ownership, board seat, and sets direction.

6. Product-Market Fit (PMF)

Before PMF: you’re experimenting.

After PMF: customers pull the product out of you — focus shifts to growth and scaling.

7. Bootstrapping

Best for founders who want steady, profitable businesses ($5–10M/year).

VC-backed startups chase hypergrowth, often sacrificing profitability for speed.

8. Convertible Notes & SAFEs

Notes: Function like a loan, often with interest, that converts later.

SAFEs: Simpler, no interest, widely used in seed rounds.

9. TAM (Total Addressable Market)

Not fixed — markets often expand as products redefine them.

Example: Uber made the ride-hailing market far larger than the old taxi market.

Example: Tesla expanded the EV market by making it aspirational.

10. Valuation

Private company valuation ≠ liquid market value.

Investors use it as a signal of progress, not always a true price.

11. IPO (Initial Public Offering)

Big milestone where a startup becomes public.

Allows founders, employees, and investors to cash out.

Usually happens once the company is stable, profitable, and trusted.

12. ARR & MRR

Recurring revenue is king: Investors value predictability.

Example: SaaS companies with $1M ARR are far more attractive than one-off sales.
